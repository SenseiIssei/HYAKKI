# 01 — The Lore

Researched, not remembered. Sources at the bottom; every claim here traces to one.

**A note on respect.** This is a living religious and folk tradition, not a monster manual.
The rule for HYAKKI: use the *shapes* and the *ideas* faithfully, invent freely inside them,
and never put a real deity in the position of a boss to be farmed. Enma judges you; you do
not kill Enma. Jizō shelters you and is never an obstacle.

---

## 1. Hyakki Yagyō 百鬼夜行 — the Night Parade

A procession of yōkai and oni that marches through the streets at night. **"Hyakki" means
countless demons; "yagyō" means walking at night.** The term appears in *Konjaku
Monogatari-shū* and other late Heian (794–1185) collections.

As an idiom it means utter pandemonium — specifically **the barrier between the human and
supernatural worlds breaking down**. Anyone caught in the parade is killed or spirited away.
One origin tale: a nobleman's house fills with a hundred yōkai while he is away; they hold a
party in it; he prays, and at dawn the light scatters them.

It is often led by **Nurarihyon**, **Nozuchi** and **Otoroshi**.

**What we take:** the Parade is the Column. You are *in* it — not fighting it. The barrier
being down is why the world is the way it is. Dawn is the only thing it fears, and dawn
never comes in Yomi.

---

## 2. Yomi 黄泉 and Izanami — the corpse you walk through

Izanami dies giving birth to **Kagutsuchi**, the fire god; her body is fatally polluted and
she goes to **Yomi**. Izanagi follows to retrieve her. She tells him she has already eaten
the food of the underworld and cannot return, and asks him not to look at her.

He looks. Her body is **rotting and infested with maggots**, with the **Eight Thunders**
protruding from it. He flees; she sends the **Yomotsu-shikome** — a hag, or eight hags — to
chase him; he seals the entrance, the **Yomotsu Hirasaka** (the Even Pass of Yomi), with a
boulder.

**What we take:** the world. HYAKKI takes place inside Izanami's body, which is still dying
and has been for a very long time. The Eight Thunders are landmarks. The sealed pass is
where every run begins — you come *back in* through it. This replaces the Hollow exactly,
and improves it: the corpse is specific, named, and has a reason to be angry.

---

## 3. Sai no Kawara 賽の河原 — the riverbed, and the loop

Souls at the riverbed stack stones into towers as penance and as merit for the living. Just
as a tower is finished, **oni come and knock it down**, and it begins again. **Jizō**
appears at the bank, hides souls in his robes, and shelters them.

Passers-by in the real world stack stones near Jizō statues to help complete the penance.

**What we take:** the prestige loop, and the game's emotional centre. Every death returns
you to the riverbed. Stacking is the reset. **The stones survive; the tower never does.**

> **Scope decision (yours):** in the folklore this is specifically a limbo for children who
> died before their parents. HYAKKI does **not** put you in that role. You are an adult soul
> stacking stones *for someone else's sake* — which is what the living do at the real
> roadside Jizō. The imagery and the loop are kept; the subject is not.

---

## 4. Sanzu no Kawa 三途の川 — the river of three crossings

The boundary between the living and the dead, met around the fourteenth day. **Three
crossings, assigned by the moral weight of your life:** a safe **bridge**, a shallow
**ford**, and a **torrent** for sinners. **Datsue-ba**, an old ogress, strips the clothes
from the dead at the bank. Beyond it lie the judges.

The dead are buried with **rokumonsen** — six coins — to pay the crossing.

**What we take:** the Descent route map, unchanged in structure but finally *motivated* —
bridge/ford/torrent is a difficulty choice made before you set off. Six coins become the
Keys. Datsue-ba takes something from you at the entrance to every Descent.

---

## 5. Jigoku 地獄 and the Ten Kings — judgment

Buddhist hell: **eight hot hells and eight cold hells**. The hot ones have burning ground,
iron mountains, boiling pots, forests of razor leaves. The cold ones are frozen voids.

Ruled by **Enma-ō** (Sanskrit Yama), who judges the dead against a register of their sins.
He is assisted by two disembodied heads: **Miru-me**, female, who perceives secret faults,
and **Kagu-hana**, male, who smells out misdeeds. Enma is one of the **Ten Kings** (十王)
who judge souls at intervals after death.

**What we take:** the Ten Kings become the ten Stand bosses, one per tier of ten Ri. The
hells become the Descent layers. Miru-me and Kagu-hana are Enma's two "guards" — the ISSUE
mechanic already in the engine, now with a reason to exist.

---

## 6. Yūrei 幽霊 — the dead who do not leave

Bound by the circumstances of their death:

| Type | Bound by |
|---|---|
| **Onryō** 怨霊 | Rage. Died wronged; returns to harm. Almost always malicious. |
| **Ubume** 産女 | Grief. Died in or near childbirth; returns to care for a child. |
| **Goryō** 御霊 | Station. Nobles who died in political ruin; blamed for plagues and bad harvests — **and quieted by being enshrined**. Not necessarily wrathful. |
| **Funayūrei** 船幽霊 | Water. Drowned at sea; sometimes scaled, fish-like. |

**What we take:** the Returned enemy family becomes yūrei, sorted by *how that run of yours
died* — a run that died at a Stand comes back onryō; one that drowned in a flooded hell
comes back funayūrei. And **goryō is the Names mechanic**: enshrining a wrathful spirit with
a name is literally how you buy things numbers cannot.

---

## 7. Tsukumogami 付喪神 — the relics

Objects that have served for **nearly one hundred years receive a soul** and become aware.
The concept dates to at least the 10th century and appears in the Muromachi-era
*Tsukumogami-ki*. Usually harmless, prone to small pranks — **but they can turn, and they
gang up on those who threw them away or used them badly.**

Classic forms: **karakasa** (one-eyed hopping umbrella), **chōchin-obake** (paper lantern
with a split face), **bakezōri** (straw sandal).

**What we take:** the entire relic system, with a better rule than rarity — **age**. A relic
is powerful because it is old, and old things have opinions. The True Name tier becomes
objects old enough to resent you. Melting a tsukumogami is not "recycling"; it is destroying
something that had begun to be a person.

---

## 8. Hyakumonogatari Kaidankai 百物語怪談会 — the archive

A parlour game: light a hundred candles, tell a hundred ghost stories, and snuff one candle
after each. At the hundredth, the room is dark, and something is supposed to arrive.

**What we take:** the fragment Archive. Every fragment is a story told and a candle put out.
The screen gets darker as you read. At one hundred — see [03](03-CONTENT.md).

---

## 9. Oni 鬼 and the wider yōkai

**Oni**: brawny ogres, red or blue skin, wild hair, horns, tiger-skin loincloths, iron
clubs. They are **tormentors of the wicked in Jigoku**, guardians who scare off evil, or
plain brutes. Note they are *staff*, not intruders — the ones at Sai no Kawara are doing a
job.

The wider category includes things that were never human: **kitsune**, **tengu**, and the
**tsukumogami** above.

**What we take:** oni are the heavy enemy family and they are *employed*. They are not
angry at you. That is worse.
